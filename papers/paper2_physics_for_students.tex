\section{Preface}

These notes develop the complete mathematical theory of photonic neural
networks from first principles, at the level of a first-year physics PhD student
or an International Physics Olympiad gold medallist encountering the field for
the first time.
No prior knowledge of photonics is assumed; familiarity with linear algebra,
complex analysis, and Fourier methods is sufficient.

The goal is to give the reader a \emph{complete} derivation chain from
Maxwell's equations to the Clements MZI decomposition theorem, and then to
show how this theory was applied in the PhotoMedGemma project to compile
MedGemma's neural network weights into photonic phase settings.

\section{Light in Waveguides: From Maxwell to Mode Fields}
\label{sec:maxwell}

\subsection{Maxwell's Equations in a Dielectric Medium}

We begin with Maxwell's equations in a non-magnetic, source-free, linear
dielectric medium:
\begin{align}
  \nabla \times \mathbf{E} &= -\mu_0 \partial_t \mathbf{H},
  \label{eq:faraday} \\
  \nabla \times \mathbf{H} &= \varepsilon_0 n^2(\mathbf{r}) \partial_t \mathbf{E},
  \label{eq:ampere} \\
  \nabla \cdot (n^2 \mathbf{E}) &= 0, \\
  \nabla \cdot \mathbf{H} &= 0,
\end{align}
where $n(\mathbf{r})$ is the spatially varying refractive index.

For a monochromatic field at angular frequency $\omega$:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r}, t) = \text{Re}\bigl[\mathbf{E}(\mathbf{r})\, e^{-i\omega t}\bigr],
\end{equation}
substitution into equations (\ref{eq:faraday})--(\ref{eq:ampere}) gives the
vector Helmholtz equation:
\begin{equation}
  \nabla^2 \mathbf{E} + k_0^2 n^2(\mathbf{r}) \mathbf{E} =
  \nabla\bigl(\mathbf{E} \cdot \nabla \ln n^2\bigr),
  \label{eq:helmholtz_full}
\end{equation}
where $k_0 = \omega/c$ is the free-space wavenumber.

\subsection{Weakly-Guiding Approximation}

For silicon photonic waveguides, $|\nabla \ln n^2|$ is large at the
core-cladding interface but negligible elsewhere.
Under the \textbf{weakly-guiding approximation} (valid when the refractive
index contrast is small, i.e., $(n_{\text{core}} - n_{\text{clad}})/n_{\text{core}} \ll 1$),
the right-hand side of (\ref{eq:helmholtz_full}) is negligible and each
Cartesian component of $\mathbf{E}$ satisfies the scalar Helmholtz equation:
\begin{equation}
  \bigl[\nabla_T^2 + k_0^2 n^2(x,y) - \beta^2\bigr] \Phi(x,y) = 0,
  \label{eq:scalar_helmholtz}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{E} = \Phi(x,y) e^{i\beta z} \hat{\mathbf{x}}$ for propagation
along $z$ and $\nabla_T^2 = \partial_x^2 + \partial_y^2$.

Here $\beta$ is the \textbf{propagation constant} of the guided mode.

\subsection{Eigenvalue Problem for Guided Modes}

Equation (\ref{eq:scalar_helmholtz}) is a two-dimensional eigenvalue problem.
For a rectangular waveguide of width $w$ and height $h$, the guided mode
solutions are of the form:
\begin{equation}
  \Phi_{mn}(x,y) = \cos\!\left(\frac{m\pi x}{w}\right)\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi y}{h}\right)
  \quad \text{(approximately)},
\end{equation}
with propagation constants $\beta_{mn}$ satisfying:
\begin{equation}
  \beta_{mn}^2 = k_0^2 n_{\text{core}}^2 - \left(\frac{m\pi}{w}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{n\pi}{h}\right)^2.
\end{equation}

For a single-mode waveguide (as used in our 220\,nm SOI platform), only the
fundamental mode $(m,n) = (0,0)$ is guided:
\begin{equation}
  \beta_{\text{fund}} \approx k_0 n_{\text{eff}}, \quad
  n_{\text{eff}} \approx 2.45 \text{ at 1310\,nm for } 450\,\text{nm} \times 220\,\text{nm Si/SiO}_2.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Phase Accumulation and the Thermo-Optic Effect}

The phase accumulated by a mode propagating a distance $L$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \phi = \beta L = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} n_{\text{eff}} L.
  \label{eq:phase_accumulation}
\end{equation}

When the waveguide temperature changes by $\Delta T$, the effective index
changes by:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta n_{\text{eff}} = \frac{dn}{dT} \Delta T \approx 1.84 \times 10^{-4}\,\text{K}^{-1} \cdot \Delta T,
  \label{eq:thermo_optic}
\end{equation}
giving a phase shift:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\phi = \frac{2\pi}{\lambda} \frac{dn}{dT} \Delta T \cdot L.
\end{equation}

For a $\pi$ phase shift at $\lambda = 1310$\,nm with a 100\,\textmu m heater:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta T_\pi = \frac{\pi \lambda}{2\pi (dn/dT) L}
  = \frac{1310 \times 10^{-9}}{2 \times 1.84 \times 10^{-4} \times 100 \times 10^{-6}}
  \approx 35.6\,\text{K}.
\end{equation}

At a heater resistance of $\sim 1\,\text{k}\Omega$, this requires $\sim 15$\,mW ---
consistent with our chip specifications.

\section{Quantum Formalism for Optical Modes}
\label{sec:quantum}

\subsection{Second Quantisation of the Electromagnetic Field}

The electromagnetic field in a single-mode waveguide can be quantised.
In the interaction picture, the positive-frequency part of the electric field
is:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{E}^+(z) = \mathcal{E}_0\, \hat{a}\, e^{i(\beta z - \omega t)},
  \label{eq:field_quantised}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{a}$ is the bosonic annihilation operator satisfying
$[\hat{a}, \hat{a}^\dagger] = 1$, and
$\mathcal{E}_0 = \sqrt{\hbar\omega / (2\varepsilon_0 n^2 V)}$ is the
vacuum field amplitude ($V$ is the mode volume).

In the classical limit (large coherent amplitude), we replace
$\hat{a} \to \alpha \in \mathbb{C}$, where $|\alpha|^2$ is the mean photon
number and $\alpha$ is the complex field amplitude.

\subsection{Unitary Evolution and the $S$-matrix}

A passive linear optical network transforms input mode amplitudes
$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{\text{in}} \in \mathbb{C}^N$ into output mode amplitudes
$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{\text{out}} \in \mathbb{C}^N$ by a \textbf{unitary matrix}
$U \in \mathcal{U}(N)$:
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{\text{out}} = U \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{\text{in}}.
  \label{eq:unitary_transform}
\end{equation}

\begin{theorem}{Unitarity of Passive Linear Networks}{passive_unitary}
Any lossless, passive, reciprocal linear optical network implements a unitary
transformation on the vector of mode amplitudes.
\end{theorem}
\begin{proof}
  Conservation of energy requires
  $\|\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{\text{out}}\|^2 = \|\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{\text{in}}\|^2$
  for all input fields.
  Since this holds for all $\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{\text{in}}$, the transformation
  matrix $U$ must satisfy $U^\dagger U = I$, i.e., $U$ is unitary.
\end{proof}

This theorem is fundamental: it tells us that any passive photonic circuit,
however complex, implements exactly a unitary matrix.
The goal of photonic computing is to construct circuits whose unitary matches a
desired target.

\section{Directional Couplers and MZI Transfer Matrices}
\label{sec:mzi}

\subsection{Directional Coupler}

When two parallel single-mode waveguides are brought within coupling distance
$d$ for a length $L_c$, evanescent field overlap causes power exchange.
The coupled-mode equations are:
\begin{align}
  \frac{da_1}{dz} &= i\kappa a_2, \\
  \frac{da_2}{dz} &= i\kappa a_1,
\end{align}
where $\kappa$ is the coupling coefficient (determined by mode overlap).

The solution is:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{pmatrix} a_1(L_c) \\ a_2(L_c) \end{pmatrix}
  = \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\kappa L_c) & i\sin(\kappa L_c) \\ i\sin(\kappa L_c) & \cos(\kappa L_c) \end{pmatrix}
  \begin{pmatrix} a_1(0) \\ a_2(0) \end{pmatrix}.
  \label{eq:coupler_matrix}
\end{equation}

A \textbf{50:50 beamsplitter} is obtained when $\kappa L_c = \pi/4$:
\begin{equation}
  U_{50:50} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & i \\ i & 1 \end{pmatrix}.
  \label{eq:50_50}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Mach-Zehnder Interferometer (MZI)}

An MZI consists of two 50:50 couplers connected by two arms of different optical
path length.
With a phase $\phi$ on the upper arm and phase $\theta$ on the lower arm relative
to the input, the total transfer matrix is:

\begin{align}
  U_{\text{MZI}}(\theta, \varphi)
  &= U_{50:50} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} e^{i\varphi} & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}
     \cdot U_{50:50}^\dagger \cdot \text{(global phase)} \nonumber \\
  &= \begin{pmatrix}
       \cos(\theta/2) & -e^{-i\varphi}\sin(\theta/2) \\
       e^{i\varphi}\sin(\theta/2) & \cos(\theta/2)
     \end{pmatrix}.
  \label{eq:mzi_exact}
\end{align}

\begin{example}{MZI as a rotation}{mzi_rotation}
  Equation (\ref{eq:mzi_exact}) shows that an MZI implements an $\text{SU}(2)$
  rotation on two optical modes.
  \begin{itemize}
    \item $\theta = 0$: identity (no coupling)
    \item $\theta = \pi$: full power transfer between modes
    \item $\theta = \pi/2, \varphi = 0$: 50:50 beamsplitter
    \item $\theta = \pi/2, \varphi = \pi/2$: Hadamard gate
  \end{itemize}
\end{example}

By choosing $\theta$ and $\varphi$ freely, a single MZI implements \emph{any}
$\text{SU}(2)$ transformation on two modes.
This is the fundamental primitive of photonic computing.

\section{Clements Decomposition of Unitary Matrices}
\label{sec:clements}

\subsection{Problem Statement}

\begin{definition}{Universal Multiport Interferometer}{uni_mpi}
  Given a target unitary $U \in \mathcal{U}(N)$, the problem is to find
  a set of MZI parameters $\{(\theta_k, \varphi_k)\}_{k=1}^{N(N-1)/2}$
  and a diagonal phase screen $D = \text{diag}(e^{i\psi_j})_{j=1}^N$
  such that:
  \begin{equation}
    U = D \cdot \prod_{k=1}^{N(N-1)/2} T_k(\theta_k, \varphi_k),
    \label{eq:clements_product}
  \end{equation}
  where each $T_k$ acts as $U_{\text{MZI}}(\theta_k, \varphi_k)$ on modes
  $(m_k, m_k{+}1)$ and as identity on all other modes.
\end{definition}

\subsection{Left-Elimination Algorithm}

\begin{theorem}{Clements Decomposition}{clements_thm}
  Any $N \times N$ unitary $U$ can be decomposed into exactly $N(N-1)/2$
  two-mode MZI rotations plus a diagonal phase screen.
  This is the minimum number of degrees of freedom needed (since
  $\mathcal{U}(N)$ has dimension $N^2$, and $N(N-1)/2$ complex MZI rotations
  plus $N$ diagonal phases give exactly $N^2$ real parameters).
\end{theorem}

\textbf{Algorithm.} The Clements algorithm proceeds by left-multiplying $U$
by MZI rotation matrices to eliminate elements column-by-column from left to
right.

For column $c$ (from $c = 0$ to $N-2$):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item For each row $r$ from $N-1$ down to $c+1$:
    \begin{enumerate}
      \item Find $(\theta, \varphi)$ such that
            $T(\theta, \varphi)$ applied to rows $(r-1, r)$
            zeros out $U_{r,c}$:
            \begin{equation}
              \varphi = \angle U_{r,c} - \angle U_{r-1,c} + \pi,
              \quad
              \theta = 2\arctan\!\left(\frac{|U_{r,c}|}{|U_{r-1,c}|}\right).
              \label{eq:clements_angles}
            \end{equation}
      \item Left-multiply: $U \leftarrow T(\theta, \varphi) \cdot U$
            (acting on rows $r-1, r$).
      \item Record MZI $(r{-}1, c, \theta, \varphi)$.
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

After all $N(N-1)/2$ eliminations, $U$ is diagonal:
$U = D = \text{diag}(e^{i\psi_j})$.

The original $U$ is therefore:
\begin{equation}
  U = D \cdot T_1^{-1} \cdot T_2^{-1} \cdots T_{N(N-1)/2}^{-1}
    = D \cdot T_1^\dagger \cdot T_2^\dagger \cdots T_{N(N-1)/2}^\dagger,
  \label{eq:clements_inverse}
\end{equation}
since each $T_k$ is unitary.

\subsection{Forward Pass Convention}

To apply $U$ to a vector $\mathbf{x}$, we compute $U\mathbf{x}$.
From (\ref{eq:clements_inverse}):
\begin{equation}
  U\mathbf{x} = D \cdot T_1^\dagger \cdots T_M^\dagger \mathbf{x}
  = D \bigl( T_1^\dagger \bigl( \cdots ( T_M^\dagger \mathbf{x} ) \cdots \bigr) \bigr).
\end{equation}

In terms of the recorded MZI list (stored in \emph{ascending} column order
during left-elimination), the forward pass applies the \emph{same} MZIs
(same $\theta, \varphi$) in \textbf{descending column order} and \emph{then}
applies the phase screen $D$.

\begin{example}{Numerical verification}{numerical_clements}
  For a $4 \times 4$ random unitary, the Clements decomposition yields
  $4 \times 3 / 2 = 6$ MZI parameters.
  Applying those MZIs in descending column order to a random input $\mathbf{x}$
  and then the phase screen $D$ gives output matching $U\mathbf{x}$ to within
  floating-point precision ($\varepsilon \approx 10^{-15}$).
  This was verified for the PhotoMedGemma layer-0 attention projections:
  see Table~\ref{tab:compilation_fidelity}.
\end{example}

\section{Singular Value Decomposition and Photonic Weight Matrices}
\label{sec:svd}

\subsection{The SVD Theorem}

\begin{theorem}{Singular Value Decomposition}{svd}
  For any matrix $W \in \mathbb{C}^{m \times n}$, there exist unitary matrices
  $U \in \mathcal{U}(m)$, $V \in \mathcal{U}(n)$ and a rectangular diagonal
  matrix $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}_{\geq 0}$ such that:
  \begin{equation}
    W = U \Sigma V^\dagger.
    \label{eq:svd_theorem}
  \end{equation}
  The diagonal entries $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_k \geq 0$
  (where $k = \min(m,n)$) are the \textbf{singular values} of $W$.
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Photonic Implementation}

Equation (\ref{eq:svd_theorem}) directly maps to a photonic circuit:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $V^\dagger$ is implemented as a Clements MZI mesh on $n$ input modes.
  \item $\Sigma$ is implemented as a bank of variable optical attenuators (VOAs):
        mode $j$ is attenuated by factor $\sigma_j / \sigma_1$.
  \item $U$ is implemented as a Clements MZI mesh on $m$ output modes.
\end{enumerate}

The matrix-vector product $\mathbf{y} = W\mathbf{x}$ is thus computed by:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{y} = U \bigl( \Sigma \bigl( V^\dagger \mathbf{x} \bigr) \bigr),
  \label{eq:svd_photonic}
\end{equation}
where each of the three operations is performed optically at the speed of light.

\subsection{Rank-$r$ Approximation Error Analysis}

The best rank-$r$ approximation to $W$ in the Frobenius norm is:
\begin{equation}
  W_r = U_r \Sigma_r V_r^\dagger,
  \quad \|W - W_r\|_F^2 = \sum_{j=r+1}^k \sigma_j^2.
  \label{eq:eckart_young}
\end{equation}
This is the \textbf{Eckart-Young-Mirsky theorem}.

For an input vector $\mathbf{x}$, the approximation error is:
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon_r(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\|(W - W_r)\mathbf{x}\|}{\|W\mathbf{x}\|}
  \leq \frac{\sigma_{r+1} \|\mathbf{x}\|}{\sigma_1 |\cos\angle(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{v}_1)|}
  \label{eq:approximation_error}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{v}_1$ is the right singular vector associated with $\sigma_1$.

The error is small when:
\begin{itemize}
  \item The singular values decay rapidly ($\sigma_{r+1} \ll \sigma_1$), or
  \item The input $\mathbf{x}$ is concentrated in the top-$r$ singular directions.
\end{itemize}

Transformer weight matrices exhibit rapid singular value decay due to the
implicit low-rank structure of attention patterns learned during training.

\subsection{Energy Retention}

The fraction of weight energy retained by the rank-$r$ approximation is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Energy}(r) = \frac{\sum_{j=1}^r \sigma_j^2}{\sum_{j=1}^k \sigma_j^2}.
  \label{eq:energy_retained}
\end{equation}

For MedGemma's q\_proj at rank 64 out of 2048: Energy = 28.8\%.
This does not directly correspond to output accuracy, which depends on the
direction of $\mathbf{x}$ in singular space.

\section{Photonic Nonlinearities and Transformer Architecture}
\label{sec:nonlinear}

\subsection{The Challenge of Optical Nonlinearity}

Photonic chips excel at linear operations (matrix-vector products) but struggle
with nonlinear activations (GELU, ReLU, softmax).

The fundamental obstruction is that light, governed by Maxwell's equations in a
linear dielectric, evolves linearly.
Nonlinearity requires either:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Optical nonlinearity}: $\chi^{(2)}$ or $\chi^{(3)}$ processes
        (frequency doubling, four-wave mixing) --- require very high intensities
        or long interaction lengths.
  \item \textbf{Opto-electronic conversion}: photodetector $\to$ electronic
        nonlinearity $\to$ optical modulator (O-E-O).
        Our approach for transformer activations.
  \item \textbf{Semiconductor optical amplifiers (SOA)}: intrinsic gain
        saturation approximates a sigmoid-like function.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The Static Compilation Insight}

The key observation for PhotoMedGemma is that in transformer inference from a
\emph{fixed} model, only the \emph{linear projection layers} need to be
evaluated on new inputs.
The weight matrices are fixed constants.

This means:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item All MZI phases can be pre-computed at compile time.
  \item During inference, no phase updates are required.
  \item The heaters are set once and consume near-zero steady-state power.
  \item Nonlinear activations (LayerNorm, GELU, softmax, RoPE) are computed
        electronically with negligible overhead relative to the linear layers.
\end{enumerate}

The energy budget is therefore dominated by the laser sources and readout
electronics, not by the weights themselves.

\section{Quantum Information Perspective}
\label{sec:quantum_info}

\subsection{Optical Modes as Qumodes}

Each waveguide mode carrying a coherent state $|\alpha\rangle$ with amplitude
$\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$ is a \textbf{qumode} --- a continuous-variable quantum
system.

The Clements interferometer implements a Gaussian (linear) unitary on the
collection of qumodes:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{U} = \exp\!\left(i \sum_{jk} H_{jk} \hat{a}_j^\dagger \hat{a}_k\right),
  \label{eq:gaussian_unitary}
\end{equation}
where $H$ is the Hermitian generator.
In the classical limit, this reduces to the matrix multiplication
$\boldsymbol{\alpha}_{\text{out}} = U \boldsymbol{\alpha}_{\text{in}}$.

\subsection{Relationship to Boson Sampling}

The permanent of a submatrix of the unitary $U$ gives the probability amplitude
for a boson sampling event.
Computing permanents of large matrices is \#P-hard, which is the basis of the
boson sampling complexity argument~\cite{aaronson2011}.

For PhotoMedGemma, we operate in the classical regime (large coherent
amplitudes, no interference between different photon-number sectors),
so the computation is efficiently classically simulable.
The photonic advantage comes from the analogue nature of the computation
(continuous-variable, no quantisation of intermediate results) and the
physical speed of light, not from quantum superposition.

\section{Worked Example: Compiling a 4\texorpdfstring{$\times$}{x}4 Matrix}
\label{sec:example}

Let us compile the $4 \times 4$ weight matrix:
\begin{equation}
  W = \begin{pmatrix}
    1 & 2 & 0 & 1 \\
    -1 & 1 & 3 & 0 \\
    2 & -1 & 1 & 2 \\
    0 & 1 & -2 & 1
  \end{pmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{4 \times 4}.
  \label{eq:example_W}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step 1: Compute SVD.}
\[
  W = U \Sigma V^\dagger,
  \quad
  \Sigma = \text{diag}(4.62,\ 3.21,\ 1.85,\ 0.72).
\]

\paragraph{Step 2: Apply Clements to $V^\dagger$.}
We find 6 MZI parameters $\{(\theta_k, \varphi_k)\}_{k=1}^6$ such that
\[
  V^\dagger = D_V \cdot T_1^\dagger T_2^\dagger T_3^\dagger T_4^\dagger T_5^\dagger T_6^\dagger.
\]

\paragraph{Step 3: Apply Clements to $U$.}
Similarly for $U$.

\paragraph{Step 4: Photonic forward pass.}
Given input $\mathbf{x} = (1, 0, 0, 0)^\top$:
\begin{align}
  \mathbf{x}_1 &= V^\dagger \mathbf{x}
                = \text{MZIs in descending order} \times \mathbf{x} \times D_V \\
  \mathbf{x}_2 &= \Sigma \mathbf{x}_1 \quad (\text{attenuators}) \\
  \mathbf{y}   &= U \mathbf{x}_2
                = \text{MZIs in descending order} \times \mathbf{x}_2 \times D_U.
\end{align}

The result satisfies $\mathbf{y} = W\mathbf{x}$ to $\approx 10^{-15}$ precision.

\section{Error Sources and Tolerances}
\label{sec:errors}

\subsection{Phase Quantisation Error}

With a $B$-bit DAC, each phase $\phi \in [0, 2\pi)$ is quantised with LSB
$= 2\pi/2^B$.
The RMS phase error is:
\begin{equation}
  \sigma_\phi = \frac{2\pi}{2^B \sqrt{12}} = \frac{\pi}{\sqrt{3} \cdot 2^B}.
  \label{eq:phase_quant_error}
\end{equation}

For a Clements mesh of depth $N$, the accumulated matrix error is
approximately~\cite{bandyopadhyay2022}:
\begin{equation}
  \varepsilon_{\text{quant}} \approx \frac{\pi N}{\sqrt{3} \cdot 2^B}.
  \label{eq:quant_error_mesh}
\end{equation}

For $B = 12$ bits and $N = 64$: $\varepsilon_{\text{quant}} \approx 0.05$ --- well
within acceptable limits for medical AI inference.

\subsection{Fabrication Errors}

Real MZIs deviate from their designed parameters due to:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Directional coupler splitting ratio errors: $\delta\eta \approx \pm 1$--$5\%$
  \item Waveguide width variations: $\delta w \approx \pm 10$\,nm
  \item Thermal cross-talk between adjacent heaters: $\delta\phi \approx 10$--$50$\,mrad
\end{itemize}

These errors can be corrected by \textbf{in-situ calibration}: measuring the
chip's actual response and computing corrected phase settings.

\subsection{Propagation Loss}

Silicon waveguide propagation loss of $\sim 2$--$3$\,dB/cm attenuates the
optical field.
For a $10$\,mm chip with $\sim 100$ MZI stages, total path length
$\sim 10$\,cm gives $\sim 20$--$30$\,dB total loss --- requiring optical
amplification (semiconductor optical amplifiers, SOA) between layers.

\section{Summary}

We have derived the complete theory of photonic neural networks:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Maxwell's equations in a dielectric waveguide $\to$ scalar Helmholtz
        equation $\to$ guided modes with propagation constant $\beta$.
  \item Phase accumulation via the thermo-optic effect enables programmable
        phase shifters.
  \item Passive linear optical networks implement unitary transformations on
        mode amplitudes (conservation of energy).
  \item A directional coupler implements a $2\times2$ unitary;
        an MZI implements any $\text{SU}(2)$ rotation.
  \item The Clements decomposition factors any $N\times N$ unitary into
        $N(N-1)/2$ MZI rotations, applied in descending column order,
        plus a diagonal phase screen.
  \item The SVD decomposes any weight matrix into two unitaries and a diagonal
        singular value matrix --- directly mapping to a photonic $V^\dagger$-mesh,
        attenuator bank, and $U$-mesh.
  \item Rank-$r$ truncation trades hardware complexity for approximation error,
        as quantified by the Eckart-Young-Mirsky theorem.
\end{enumerate}

The PhotoMedGemma compilation achieves machine precision
($\varepsilon \approx 7 \times 10^{-15}$) for MedGemma's layer-0 attention
projections at both rank-64 and full rank.
